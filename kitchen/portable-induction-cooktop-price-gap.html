What Is a Portable Induction Cooktop?

A portable induction cooktop is a single or dual-burner cooking plate that uses electromagnetic induction to heat cookware directly. You place it on a countertop, plug it into a standard outlet, and it heats food faster and more precisely than gas or electric coil alternatives.

The appeal isn't niche — it's broad:

  • Renters: No installation, no landlord approval. Just plug in and cook.
  • Campers and van lifers: Compact, powerful (up to 1800W on 110V), runs off standard outlets.
  • Small kitchens: Adds a second cooking surface without modifying cabinetry or hiring an electrician.
  • Temperature precision: Induction offers precise low-heat control — better than most gas for simmering, sous vide backup, or delicate sauces.

The critical technical constraint is wattage vs voltage. Most US-standard portable units run at 110V/120V with 1300W–1800W. Most EU/Asian models run at 220V/240V with 2000W–3000W. Ordering the wrong voltage is the most common and costly mistake buyers make.

What Drives the Gap?

1. Brand and retail distribution costs

The $65 Amazon single burner isn't just the cooktop — it's the brand's marketing budget, Amazon's referral fee (~15%), and retail packaging amortized across unit sales. A China factory selling direct to AliExpress has none of those line items. The product inside the box is often functionally equivalent or identical.

2. Wattage certification for US voltage

This is the most underrated part of the price gap. Producing a reliable 110V/1800W induction unit that passes UL or ETL certification costs real money. Not all AliExpress sellers have this — and sellers who do typically charge $30–$45, not $20. Buyers who pay $20 for a "1800W 110V" unit and find it barely boils water have usually bought a unit with inflated wattage specs.

3. Build quality and coil efficiency

Induction heating efficiency depends on the copper coil and inverter electronics. Cheap units use thinner coils and less precise inverters, resulting in uneven heating and shorter lifespan. Mid-range AliExpress units ($28–$42) generally hit a reasonable quality floor. Below $22, quality confidence drops significantly.

Red flag to watch: If a "1800W 110V" unit is priced at $20–$22, be skeptical. Genuine 1800W at 110V requires a quality inverter and coil that can't realistically be manufactured and shipped at that price point. Verify wattage from verified buyer reviews, not just the listing spec.

How to Pick the Right Model

Before clicking, answer three questions:

1. What voltage do you need?

110V = US, Canada, Japan (standard outlet). 220V = EU, UK, Australia, most of Asia. Ordering the wrong voltage means the unit won't perform at spec — or at all.

2. What wattage do you need?

1300W: fine for simmering, light cooking, backup surface. 1500W: good daily-use cooking. 1800W: equivalent to a gas burner, handles most home cooking. 2000W+: professional or outdoor use.

3. What cookware do you have?

Induction requires magnetic cookware. Cast iron and magnetic stainless steel work. Aluminum, copper, and standard glass do not. Test your existing pans with a magnet on the bottom — if it sticks, they'll work.

FAQ

Is a $25 induction cooktop from AliExpress actually any good?

A $25 single-burner unit can be fine for light use — keeping soup warm, slow cooking, a backup surface. For daily cooking at higher heats (stir-frying, searing), the quality gap between a $25 and a $38–$42 unit is significant. Buy the $25–$30 option as a test; upgrade if you use it daily and want consistent power.

Does induction actually work better than gas?

For most home cooks in controlled environments: yes, induction is preferable. Faster heat-up, more precise temperature control, no open flame, and less ambient heat in the kitchen. Gas wins for open-wok cooking, but most apartment kitchens don't have that option anyway. The main tradeoff is needing compatible (magnetic) cookware.

What happens if I buy a 220V model and use it in the US?

It will not perform as rated — likely outputting 800–1200W equivalent heat on a 110V outlet, if it works meaningfully at all. It won't permanently damage the unit to try, but it won't meet the wattage specification. Always match voltage to your outlet before purchasing.
