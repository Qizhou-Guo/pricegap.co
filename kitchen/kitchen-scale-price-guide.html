Kitchen Scales: $12 vs $35 — The BIFL Math That Might Surprise You

A question popped up on Reddit's BuyItForLife community: "You paid $35 for your kitchen scale on Amazon. How long did it last?" The answers were... all over the place. Some people swore their OXO had been going strong for eight years. Others replaced a $25 model three times in two years. Meanwhile, the same functional spec — 0.1g precision, 11lb capacity, pull-out display — sits on AliExpress for $8–12. So who's right? And which side of the BIFL argument actually makes the better buy?

TL;DR
  • A decent kitchen scale on AliExpress costs $8–15. The equivalent on Amazon US (OXO Good Grips, Escali Primo) runs $25–$40. That's a 2–3× markup.
  • For basic baking use (0.1g precision, 11lb capacity): the AE value floor is solid. Most mid-range AE scales outperform their Amazon budget competition.
  • For precision weighing (0.01g, coffee/tea dosing, jewelry): the $12–$20 AE tier handles tasks that used to require a $40–$60 professional unit.
  • The BIFL argument cuts both ways: a $35 OXO lasting 8 years beats an $8 AE scale replaced three times. But at $10 vs $35, you'd need to buy the AE version 3+ times before the economics flip.
  • The winning play: buy the $10–$15 AE tier with strong review volume, verify the unit count (0.1g or 0.01g), and pocket the $20+ difference.

The $35 Question on r/BuyItForLife

The thread got hundreds of responses. A few patterns emerged:

  • Brand-name believers (OXO, Escali, Tanita): typically paid $28–$45, most reported 5–10 years of daily use.
  • Budget switchers: kept replacing $12–$20 Amazon basics every 1–2 years due to display failure or drifting calibration.
  • The lurkers: had been watching AliExpress options but weren't sure if the jump from $12 to $35 was worth the BIFL insurance.

The conversation glossed over something important: the mid-tier AliExpress option ($10–$15) occupies a different category than the $12 Amazon basics. It's not the same product at a different price — it's a better product than the $20 Amazon budget tier, at a lower price.

What Drives the Gap?

1. Brand and retail distribution

OXO and Escali are established consumer brands with packaging, retail shelf placement, Amazon sponsored slots, and customer service overhead. None of that exists for an AliExpress seller shipping direct from China. The product inside the scale platform is the same load cell technology — the marketing and distribution cost is the primary price delta.

2. Load cell quality and calibration drift

The heart of any digital scale is the load cell — the strain gauge that converts weight to a digital reading. Cheap load cells drift over time, giving increasingly inaccurate readings. Better load cells (used in mid-tier AE and name-brand models) hold calibration longer. The $8–$15 AE tier typically uses mid-quality load cells that outperform the $15–$22 Amazon basics — and compete with $28–$36 OXO for most baking use cases.

3. After-purchase support

Name-brand scales have warranties (OXO offers a "better than warranty" lifetime policy on many items). AE scales typically have 15–30 day return windows and limited support. This is real value — but the AE price gap is often large enough that replacing a $10 scale once is still cheaper than buying a $35 OXO.

The BIFL Math — Who Actually Wins?

The "buy it for life" argument sounds compelling until you run the numbers:

  • OXO scenario: $35 once, 8 years = $4.38/year
  • AE scale scenario: $10 initial + $10 replacement at year 3 + $10 replacement at year 6 = $30 over 6 years = $5.00/year
  • AE scale + 1 replacement: $20 over 5 years = $4.00/year

The economics are closer than the brand-loyalists suggest — and if the AE scale lasts 4+ years, it wins on cost. The real risk is quality variance: not all AE scales are equal, and a lemon from a low-rated seller can die in months. The solution: buy from sellers with 500+ reviews on a specific SKU, not just a high store rating.

How to Pick the Right Scale

1. What precision do you need?

1g precision is fine for: portioning, general cooking, weight tracking. 0.1g precision is required for: baking (yeast measurements), coffee dosing, any recipe where small quantities matter. 0.01g precision is for: espresso calibration, tea weighing, jewelry/collectibles. Don't pay for precision you don't use.

2. What capacity do you need?

5kg / 11lb covers most home baking and cooking — flour bags, large quantities, whole loaves. 10kg / 22lb for serious bakers or large-batch cooking. 200–500g only for precision espresso or jewelry work.

3. Pull-out display vs. built-in

Built-in displays get obscured when weighing large bowls or tall containers. A pull-out or rotating display (standard on most $10+ AE scales and the OXO Good Grips) solves this entirely. It's a $3–5 feature upgrade that changes daily usability — and it's essentially free on mid-tier AE models.

FAQ

Is a $10 kitchen scale from AliExpress actually accurate?

A mid-tier AE scale with 0.1g precision and strong reviews will be accurate enough for virtually all home cooking and baking. The calibration drift concern is real for cheap models — but the $8–$15 tier uses better load cells than the $12–$20 Amazon basics. For precision baking (measuring yeast to the tenth of a gram), the $10 AE tier is more than adequate.

What's the main failure mode for cheap scales?

Display failure (dead or flickering LCD) and calibration drift are the two most common issues. Both are more common in the sub-$8 AE tier and the $10–$18 Amazon basics. Spending $10–$15 on AE or $25–$30 on Escali/OXO meaningfully reduces both risks.

Should I buy OXO or the AliExpress equivalent?

If you've had good luck with OXO and value the warranty and return policy, the $28–$36 OXO Good Grips is a solid buy — it genuinely does last longer on average. But if you're price-sensitive or buying a gift (you don't know their long-term habits), the $10–$12 AE tier is the smarter call. The economics only flip if the OXO lasts 3× longer than replacing an AE scale twice.

Do I need 0.01g precision?

Only if you're calibrating espresso machines, weighing micro-doses of supplements, or working with jewelry. For standard baking, cooking, and meal prep, 0.1g precision is more than sufficient — and 0.01g scales are significantly more expensive on both AE and Amazon.

Bottom Line

The kitchen scale price gap is real and actionable — a 2–3× markup between AliExpress and Amazon for the same functional spec. The winning strategy isn't to buy the cheapest option; it's to buy the mid-tier AE scale ($10–$15) that outperforms budget Amazon models at a lower price.

The BIFL argument for OXO is emotionally appealing but financially thin: the math requires the OXO to last 3× longer than replacing a $10 AE scale twice — a high bar for a product category where both brands are using similar underlying technology.

For most home bakers: buy the $10–$14 pull-out display AE scale, verify the seller has 500+ reviews on that specific SKU, and pocket the $20 difference. If it lasts 5 years, you've spent less per year than an OXO owner.
