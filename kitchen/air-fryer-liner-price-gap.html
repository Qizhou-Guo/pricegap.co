Why a ¥15 Air Fryer Liner Costs $13–$25 Overseas

At first glance, a silicone air fryer liner looks too cheap and too simple to be interesting. But that's exactly why it's a strong price-gap product: low complexity, high demand, clear use case — and a surprisingly wide markup once it crosses into overseas retail.

TL;DR

In China, air fryer silicone liners can be sourced at roughly ¥15. Overseas, similar products routinely sell for $13–$25. That's a markup of roughly 6–12×, driven by branding, packaging, marketplace economics, and consumer convenience positioning.

Why This Product Matters

Not every product deserves a price-gap article. This one does.

Air fryer liners sit in a sweet spot: they're easy to understand, cheap enough to buy on impulse, and tied to a real everyday annoyance — cleaning. That makes them useful not just as a product, but as a lens into how modern retail pricing works.

  • Simple product: no technical education required
  • Clear pain point: less mess, faster cleanup
  • Standardized format: easier cross-platform price comparison
  • Strong content hook: “why does this $2 thing cost $20?”

What Exactly Is an Air Fryer Liner?

A silicone air fryer liner is a reusable, heat-resistant insert placed inside the fryer basket. Its job is simple: prevent food residue from sticking, reduce cleanup time, and make repeated use feel easier and less annoying.

That utility sounds minor. In retail, minor convenience often sells at a major premium.

Why the Markup Exists

This isn't just a story about “middlemen making money.” The markup survives because the product fits multiple profitable retail patterns at once.

  • Convenience premium: shoppers happily pay more to solve a small recurring annoyance
  • Marketplace framing: better photography, better bundles, cleaner positioning
  • Cross-border friction: shipping, returns, trust, and platform guarantees all add cost
  • Impulse-friendly price psychology: $13–$25 feels “reasonable” even if factory cost is tiny

In other words: the price gap isn't accidental. It's built into how low-ticket utility products get marketed abroad.
